## Section 1: Accreditation — SACSCOC, HLC, WASC

### What the standards actually say

Emory is accredited by SACSCOC (Southern Association of Colleges and Schools Commission on Colleges), which governs 11 southeastern states. The operative standard is **Core Requirement 12.1 — Academic and Student Support Services**:

> "The institution provides appropriate academic and student support programs, services, and activities consistent with its mission."

The standard is **intentionally broad and performance-based** — no specific counselor-to-student ratios, no required clinic hours, no named technology mandates. Compliance is demonstrated narratively in a Compliance Certification Report (CCR) and evaluated holistically during a 10-year on-site review.

HLC (North Central region) and WSCUC (Western region) operate under equivalent frameworks. None of the three regional bodies specify technology or digital health requirements.

### Emory's current accreditation status

SACSCOC formally reaffirmed Emory's accreditation in December 2024 with **no additional monitoring reports required** — a clean pass through 2034. Emory's required Quality Enhancement Plan (QEP) focused on experiential learning ("Connect-Integrate-Reflect"), not student wellness. This means Emory is not currently under any accreditation pressure related to health services.

### When accreditation does create pressure

Accreditation becomes a real forcing function when:
1. An institution selects **student mental health as its QEP topic** — this commits the institution to measurable outcomes, documented evidence, and a 5-year implementation plan. MedEase would be directly relevant as a documented intervention.
2. A school is **on SACSCOC monitoring status** and must demonstrate systemic support improvements. Saint Augustine's University (removed from SACSCOC membership in December 2024, primarily for financial instability) illustrates how accreditation threats become institutional crises.
3. A **specialized accreditor** (e.g., for counseling, social work, or medical programs) sanctions an institution, triggering a SACSCOC review.

### Verdict for MedEase

**Pressure level for Emory: Low.** Emory is clean through 2034. Accreditation is not a short-term forcing function. However, for schools preparing for reaffirmation cycles or currently on monitoring status, MedEase provides documentable evidence of expanded access — exactly the type of narrative evidence that SACSCOC reviewers evaluate. Sell signal: not for Emory right now, but useful for the broader market.

## Section 2: Federal Compliance — Title IX, ADA, Section 504

### The regulatory landscape

Three federal laws converge on student mental health at every university:

- **Title IX (1972):** Prohibits sex-based discrimination. Mental health intersects through: survivors of assault requiring accommodations, leave-of-absence policies applied inequitably, and retaliation complaints.
- **Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973):** Bars disability discrimination at all federally funded institutions. Mental health diagnoses — depression, anxiety, PTSD, ADHD, eating disorders — are covered disabilities. Requires "reasonable accommodations" without undue burden.
- **ADA (1990, expanded 2008):** Extends protections to private universities. The 2008 ADAAA broadened covered disability definitions significantly. HHS finalized major Section 504 regulatory updates in May 2024, adding explicit digital accessibility and communication standards.

### How enforcement actually works

The U.S. Department of Education's **Office for Civil Rights (OCR)** is the enforcement body. OCR does not levy direct fines. The mechanism:
1. A student files a complaint (complaint volume has risen sharply — OCR received 19,000+ complaints in FY2024)
2. OCR investigates; most cases resolve during investigation with **resolution agreements** requiring systemic program changes
3. The nuclear option — **termination of federal funding** — is rarely used but legally available and existentially threatening

**For Emory specifically:** Emory receives hundreds of millions in federal research grants and financial aid annually. OCR non-compliance is not a theoretical risk — it is an existential one.

### What non-compliance actually costs

| Cost category | Estimated range |
|---|---|
| OCR investigation defense (legal) | $1M–$3M before settlement |
| Title IX settlement (documented cases) | $0–$4.1M (UMBC: $4.14M DOJ settlement) |
| ADA/504 remediation (digital accessibility) | $50K–$500K+ per incident |
| Disability Services Office (ongoing) | $150K–$800K/year (staffing) |
| Liberty University Title IX compliance investments | $8.5M (security infrastructure) |

In 2025, OCR published 165 resolutions through mid-year. Of 35 higher-education disability cases, **32 required systemic program changes** during the investigation phase — meaning compliance costs were incurred even without a formal finding of violation.

### The specific OCR gap MedEase addresses

OCR's disability complaint investigations repeatedly identify one pattern: **institutions that acknowledge a student's disability but fail to provide accommodations in a timely manner.** The standard is not just "did you have accommodations?" — it is "did you provide them on time?"

MedEase directly compresses the critical path:
- Student identifies a health need → MedEase routes them to the correct service within minutes rather than days
- Student seeking disability accommodations → MedEase guides them to the SDS (Student Disability Services) pathway immediately
- Documentation of navigation activity → institutions can show OCR a timestamped record of proactive student support

A 2024 California civil rights case resulted in a **$230,000+ settlement** after an institution failed to engage in the required interactive accommodation process for a graduate student. The failure was not a refusal — it was a delay and procedural breakdown. MedEase reduces precisely this type of friction.

### Verdict for MedEase

**Pressure level: Moderate–High, nationally uniform.** Unlike accreditation pressure (which varies by reaffirmation cycle) or state mandates (which vary by geography), OCR complaint risk exists at every federally funded institution in every year. The argument to a university CFO or General Counsel is: "MedEase reduces the procedural friction that generates OCR complaints, and we can show you the timestamped data."

## Section 3: State Mental Health Mandates

### California — SB 11 (signed law, hard metrics)

> **Note:** The commonly cited "SB 768" is a CalWORKs financial aid bill, not a campus mental health bill. The correct California campus mental health law is **SB 11 (2023–2024).**

**What SB 11 requires (California State Universities):**
- Minimum **1 FTE licensed counselor per 1,500 enrolled students** on each CSU campus
- Reporting to the Legislature by January 1, 2025, and **every three years thereafter** on:
  - Number of mental health counselors employed per campus
  - **Student wait times for mental health counseling appointments**
- Funding appropriated annually for compliance

**Current compliance gap:** As of fall 2020, CSU's systemwide ratio was **1,958 students per counselor** — out of compliance with the 1:1,500 standard. Closing this gap requires significant new hires or technology-based capacity extensions.

**Why this matters for MedEase:** Wait time is now a **legislatively mandated, publicly reported metric** for CSU institutions. A tool that demonstrably reduces wait time for first appointments does not just improve student experience — it directly improves a number that goes to the legislature. This is the clearest statutory demand signal for any health navigation product in the U.S. university market.

### New York — S1008 and pending staffing ratio bills (2025 session)

**NY S1008 (2025 Legislative Session) — pending:**
- Requires SUNY, CUNY, and independent institutions to review and amend mental health protocols
- Requires each college president to appoint an **advisory committee on mental health**
- Committees must report in writing to the president **at least once per academic year**, with reports available upon public request
- Requires parity between physical and mental health policies

**Pending NY staffing ratio bill:**
- Would require **1 clinical mental health staff member per 1,000 students** at all SUNY/CUNY campuses

**Penalty structure:** No direct financial penalties in current NY bills. Enforcement through institutional reporting obligations to the Commissioner and political/reputational pressure. State appropriations to SUNY/CUNY institutions could theoretically be conditioned on compliance.

### Georgia — What applies to Emory

Georgia has **no state-level campus mental health staffing mandate** equivalent to California's SB 11 or New York's pending ratio bills.

Relevant Georgia legislation:
- **HB 1013 (2022):** Requires health insurers to report on mental health parity compliance. Affects Emory's student health insurance plan design, but not direct service delivery mandates.
- **SB 480:** Student loan repayment for mental health professionals in underserved areas — a workforce pipeline measure, not a campus mandate.

**What Emory has done voluntarily:**
- **March 2024:** Emory CAPS expanded to **24/7 support**, with students able to reach a licensed mental health professional immediately by phone (404-727-7450)
- **TimelyCare partnership:** Virtual health services extended to students outside Georgia, available after hours
- Emory Student Health Services provides **free psychiatric services** alongside counseling

This voluntary expansion signals institutional commitment — but in the absence of state mandates, the budget pressure is internally generated, not externally imposed.

### Verdict for MedEase

**Pressure level: Low for Emory, High for CA/NY schools.** Georgia's minimal regulatory environment means MedEase cannot be sold to Emory as a compliance tool. However, for the national market:
- California CSU schools face a hard staffing ratio and wait-time reporting mandate — MedEase is close to a compliance necessity
- New York institutions face pending mandates — early adoption positions them ahead of the compliance curve
- Georgia/Southeast schools are the weakest regulatory market for this argument

## Section 4: Rankings — Princeton Review, U.S. News, NSSE

### Princeton Review: Health services are a named ranking category

**The Princeton Review explicitly ranks institutions on health and counseling services.** The 2026 rankings include:
- **"Best Health Services"** — named ranked list (University of Virginia held #1 for multiple consecutive years; Emory does not appear in the top 10)
- **"Best Student Support and Counseling Services"** — separate ranked list (Seton Hall #1 in 2026)
- **Mental Health Services Honor Roll (2026):** 30 schools named (doubled from 16 in 2025), based on the annual Campus Mental Health Services Survey

**Methodology:** 100% student surveys. ~170,000 students surveyed annually across 89 questions. Health-specific questions ask students to rate the quality and accessibility of health services and counseling. The ranking is purely student perception — not accreditor assessments, not staffing ratios.

**2025–2026 Campus Mental Health Services Survey key findings:**
- **81% of college applicants** say mental health service information influences their college decision
- 76% of students agree mental health services are readily available on their campus (up 5% year-over-year)
- 66% believe their college prioritizes student mental health (up 5%)
- Survey conducted across 540 institutions; 31,000+ students across 300 colleges

**The enrollment math for Emory:**
- Emory undergraduate enrollment: ~7,000 students
- Annual tuition: ~$62,000
- If 81% of applicants weight mental health services in their decision, and Emory's Princeton Review health ranking improves enough to convert even 1% more of admitted students:
  - Emory's typical undergraduate class: ~1,400 students
  - 1% yield improvement = ~14 additional enrolled students
  - Annual tuition revenue impact: **~$868,000/year**
  - Over 4 years (cohort lifetime): **~$3.5M**

This is a conservative estimate — it excludes room/board, fees, and graduate program conversion.

### U.S. News: Indirect but financially dominant

Health services are **not a named ranking factor** in U.S. News methodology. The 2026 methodology weights:
- Student Outcomes: **52%** (graduation rates 16%, retention 5%, graduation rate performance 10%, Pell metrics 11%, debt + earnings 10%)
- Reputation (peer assessment): **20%**
- Faculty Resources: **11–15%**
- Financial Resources (per-student spending): **8%**
- Student Selectivity: **5%**

However, the **indirect path is operationally real:**
- **Retention (5% direct weight) + graduation rates (26% combined weight) = 31% of the ranking is downstream of student persistence**
- Mental health is the #1 reported driver of student departure: EAB research shows **55% of students who consider leaving cite emotional stress as the primary reason**
- TimelyCare partner universities show a **1.3 percentage point above-expected retention lift** after implementing virtual mental health access; reported ROI is 5x
- For Emory (7,000 students × $62,000 tuition × 1.3pp retention improvement):
  - ~91 additional students retained per year
  - **Annual tuition revenue preserved: ~$5.6M**

NBER research confirms ranking changes have real financial consequences: U.S. News improvements lead to higher state appropriations per student at public universities, better yield rates, and reduced discount rates (institutions offer less grant aid to attract students).

**What U.S. News does capture from per-student spending:** Spending on student services is included in the "financial resources" metric (8% weight). A well-resourced health and counseling center marginally improves this metric.

### NSSE

NSSE measures educational engagement, not health service quality. It is used internally for institutional improvement, not by U.S. News for rankings. It has marginal relevance as a competitive differentiation signal — schools that publish strong NSSE "campus environment" scores sometimes highlight them in admissions materials — but it is not a meaningful demand driver for MedEase.

### Verdict for MedEase

**Pressure level: Moderate–High, nationally applicable.** The Princeton Review path is the clearest: improve the student-reported health service experience → improve the score on a named ranking category that 81% of applicants say influences their decision. The U.S. News path is indirect but financially dominant: mental health access → retention → 26–31% of the ranking score. Both paths point to the same underlying mechanism — students who get faster, more accessible health navigation stay enrolled longer and rate their experience higher.

## Verdict: Does the Hospital Reimbursement Penalty Analogy Hold at Universities?

**Conditional — with important structural differences.**

The hospital analogy (CMS withholds reimbursement for missing metrics) works cleanly in one state: California. SB 11 creates a hard metric (wait time), a hard ratio (1:1,500), and a legislative reporting cycle — all structurally similar to a CMS quality measure. A California CSU that misses the wait-time standard faces a politically embarrassing public report and potential appropriations pressure.

For the rest of the U.S., the analogy is weaker. The pressure is real but comes through:
- **Reputational loss** (Princeton Review rankings, applicant perception) rather than direct financial penalty
- **Indirect financial loss** (lower yield, higher discount rates, lower retention revenue) rather than a payment clawback
- **OCR investigation costs** (legal defense + remediation) rather than a per-metric fine

The penalty exists — it's just distributed differently. Universities don't get a bill for "failing to reduce wait times." They lose enrollment, lose retention, and spend legal fees. The business case for MedEase is real; it's just told through a different causal chain than hospital reimbursement.

**The single strongest metric:** Student wait time for first mental health appointment.
- Mandated by CA SB 11 (legislatively reported)
- Proxied in Princeton Review student surveys (rankings impact)
- Central to OCR "timely accommodation" standard (compliance impact)
- Directly affected by a navigation tool that connects students faster

**The single strongest buyer:** Student Affairs VP at a California CSU or large private university preparing for a Princeton Review ranking improvement campaign.

## Section 6: Student Health Insurance Plan (SHIP) Revenue

### The mechanism

Many universities offer **mandatory Student Health Insurance Plans (SHIPs)** — students are automatically enrolled and pay premiums unless they waive with proof of comparable coverage. Emory's SHIP is administered through Aetna Student Health with a 2024–2025 annual premium of approximately $3,600/student.

**The financial logic:**
- The university (or its health services arm) is effectively the plan sponsor
- Lower claims = better plan loss ratio = ability to reduce premiums (enrollment differentiator) or retain a surplus
- Unnecessary ER visits are among the highest-cost claims: average ER visit cost $1,500–$3,000; avoidable ER visits driven by lack of access to primary or mental health care

**What MedEase moves:**
- **ER diversion:** A student who might go to the Emory ER for a panic attack at 11 PM instead reaches CAPS 24/7 or TimelyCare through MedEase navigation → $2,000 claim averted
- **Appropriate care utilization:** Students using urgent care ($150–$300) vs. ER ($1,500–$3,000) for equivalent acuity levels
- **Claims frequency:** Students who access preventive mental health support earlier generate fewer high-cost crisis-level claims

**Who owns this budget:** Both the **Health Center Director** (operational cost savings) and the **CFO** (plan P&L). This is a budget line that neither typically connects to a digital navigation tool — making it a differentiated pitch angle.

**Comparable data:** Telehealth platforms broadly report 20–30% ER diversion rates for virtual care users. Applied to a university with 7,000 insured students, even modest ER diversion at 100 events/year = **$150,000–$300,000 in annual claims savings**.

## Section 7: Medical Leave and Tuition Refund Liability

### The mechanism

When a student takes a medical leave or involuntary withdrawal due to mental health crisis, the institution typically issues a **partial or full tuition refund** (depending on timing in the semester), plus room and board refunds. At Emory, tuition is approximately $62,000/year — a mid-semester medical leave can trigger a refund of $15,000–$31,000 per student.

This is a direct financial liability — but the legal exposure compounds it.

### The Yale precedent

In a documented case, **Yale University faced a class action lawsuit** in which students alleged the institution discriminated against students with mental health disabilities and **pressured them into involuntary withdrawal** when they sought mental health care. The case alleged Yale's policies violated the ADA and Section 504 by making students choose between receiving mental health treatment and remaining enrolled.

This class action represents a structural liability: if a university's response to mental health disclosure is withdrawal pressure (even implicitly), it faces:
- Class action litigation costs ($1M+ in legal defense alone)
- OCR investigation trigger (ADA/504 overlap)
- Reputational damage compounding into enrollment decline

**MedEase's intervention point:** A tool that helps students navigate to care *before they reach crisis* reduces the probability of the involuntary withdrawal pathway entirely. The student who gets a same-day telehealth appointment through MedEase navigation does not become the student who is pulled from class at week 10.

### The quantified liability

- Emory's medical leave rate is not publicly disclosed, but national data suggests 2–5% of students take some form of medical leave annually
- At 7,000 students, 2% = 140 students; at an average partial refund of $20,000 = **$2.8M in annual refund exposure**
- Even a 10% reduction in medical leave incidence through earlier intervention = **$280,000/year in avoided refunds**, before legal costs

## Section 8: Clery Act / Campus Safety Reporting

### The mechanism

The **Jeanne Clery Disclosure of Campus Security Policy and Campus Crime Statistics Act** requires all federally funded universities to:
- Collect and report annually on campus crime, arrests, and referrals for disciplinary action
- Issue timely warnings for ongoing threats
- Report emergency response procedures

Mental health intersects through: **suicide attempts, self-harm incidents, and emotionally disturbed person (EDP) responses** that are logged as campus safety events. While Clery does not directly require mental health incident reporting, **campus police responses to mental health crises** generate Clery-reportable events and are disclosed in the Annual Security Report (ASR).

**The public accountability mechanism:** Every university's ASR is publicly available. A spike in EDP calls, mental health crisis responses, or ambulance dispatches is visible to:
- Prospective students and parents (Princeton Review's 81% who research mental health services)
- Journalists (campus safety reports are commonly analyzed by student newspapers)
- Rankings researchers

This is not a financial clawback — but it is a **measurable public metric** that university administrators actively manage. A Clery-reportable mental health crisis incident rate is exactly the kind of metric a Dean of Students tracks and wants to reduce.

**MedEase's intervention:** Earlier navigation to care → fewer crisis escalations → fewer EDP calls → lower Clery-reportable incident counts. The causal chain is longer than retention ROI, but the metric is real and observable.
