# Browsing vs. Searching: UX Patterns in Unfamiliar Domains

> Added: 2026-04-22
> Relevant to: information architecture, positioning, resource hub design

---

## Core Insight

Users in **unfamiliar domains** default to browsing, not searching — because they
don't yet have the vocabulary to form a precise query. This is structurally true
for most DAS-navigating students: they don't know what accommodations exist, what
the process is called, or what to ask for.

Forcing search as the primary entry point in this context creates friction and
increases the chance of failed sessions (no results, wrong results, vague results).

---

## Implications for MedEase

- A **resource hub** framing (browsable categories, structured content) lowers
  the cognitive entry cost. Students can orient before they query.
- **AI-powered search** should be the upgrade path — available when a user has
  enough context to ask a specific question, or when browsing hasn't resolved their need.
- The two modes reinforce each other: browsing builds vocabulary, which enables
  better search queries, which produces more useful AI responses.

---

## Supporting Research

- **Nielsen Norman Group** has documented extensively that users in unfamiliar
  information spaces prefer to browse category structures over open-ended search.
  Source: nngroup.com — multiple studies on search vs. navigation behavior.
- **Information foraging theory** (Pirolli & Card, 1999) models how users follow
  "information scent" — browsable structures provide scent cues; blank search boxes
  do not.
- Healthcare UX specifically: patients and students in health contexts exhibit
  lower search confidence and higher reliance on navigation structures than general
  web users. (See: healthcare.gov and university health portal UX audits.)
